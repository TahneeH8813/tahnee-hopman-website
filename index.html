Editor

Tahnee Hopman

I edit the writing that has to hold up: board reports, investor analysis, expert publications, and the magazines and campaigns that carry an organisation's name. Eighteen years in, and I still can't walk past a misplaced hyphen. That's maybe half the job. The other half is knowing which four hundred words of a chief executive's three thousand actually make an argument, and having the nerve to cut the rest.

Two kinds of editing, one set of instincts

Most of my career has run on two tracks that turn out to be the same skill. One is leading editorial strategy for organisations that need their content to sound like them, consistently, across a magazine, a website, a podcast and a stakeholder who wants everything in by Friday. The other is quieter and more forensic: editing technical and analytical reports for readers who will notice if a number in the executive summary doesn't match the number in table four. Both come down to the same question: does this actually say what you meant it to say, to the people who are going to read it closely?

I've spent the past few years doing both at once, and I've stopped thinking of them as separate disciplines. They're the same editorial instinct, just working at different distances from the page.
