About

Eighteen years, two continents, one habit I can't switch off

I started as a features journalist in Colombo, chasing human interest stories and writing about everything from architecture to environmental policy for The Sunday Times. It was a good place to learn that a story only works if you can say, quickly, why anyone other than the person living it should care.

From there the work got more technical and considerably less romantic, in the best way. At Moody's Analytics I spent four years editing research and internal content for investment banks and hedge funds, which is where I learned to read a sentence for what it actually claims, not what it sounds like it claims. That distinction has stayed useful in every job since.

I spent the next several years running editorial for corporate publications: custom magazines for clients like Bank of Melbourne and Tabcorp at Niche Media, then close to five years at CPA Australia on INTHEBLACK, the organisation's flagship business magazine, first as deputy editor and then as editor. That job meant a monthly 74 to 80-page publication, a companion website, podcasts and video, and stakeholders across Australia and Asia-Pacific who all had opinions about the cover story. One of the special editions I produced, on workplace mental health during the pandemic, won a Mumbrella Publish Award. Another, on the future of accounting, became the organisation's best-performing piece of content.

After that I moved to RSL Victoria as editorial and communications lead, running Mufti (the member magazine), the website, the Annual Report, and the content and communications around ANZAC Day, Remembrance Day, and the ANZAC and Poppy Appeals. I launched the organisation's first podcast, Beyond the Barracks, and helped reposition its public voice following the Royal Commission into Defence and Veteran Suicide. The 2024 ANZAC Appeal grew online donations 35% year on year, work that was shortlisted for a Fundraising Excellence Award.

These days that same range of work continues, distributed a little differently. I do editorial strategy, ghostwriting and structural editing for organisations that need their content to sound consistently like them, and I do close analytical and technical editing for expert publications, currently including EnergyQuest's quarterly reports on the Australian energy sector, read by industry leaders, investors and government. The two jobs look different from the outside. From where I sit, they're the same discipline, applied at different distances: get close enough to catch the number that doesn't match, and step back far enough to see whether the argument actually holds together.

A few things about how I work

In case it's useful before we talk: I query rather than guess when I'm out of my depth on a technical point. I'll tell you when a sentence is fine and doesn't need my opinion, which apparently surprises people. And I have strong, mostly private views on the Oxford comma that I'm happy to keep to myself unless you ask.
